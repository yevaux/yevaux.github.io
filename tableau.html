Tableau Help Evaluation

Usability Study

Summary

BACKGROUND

Tableau develops and distributes interactive data visualization software to help its customers store, analyze, and explain data.


Tableau products provide a large selection of services, including mapping functionality, custom geocoding, real-time operations, and data cleaning. Users can purchase Tableau Desktop, Tableau Server, and Tableau Online. Further, Tableau Reader and Tableau Public are available for free. A disadvantage of this tremendous range is that users may be confused when seeking help.


When users have difficulty with a Tableau product, they often search online (e.g., Google) for support rather than first trying the product's built-in Help function. An advantage of the built-in Help is that it is always written for the user's Tableau version. For users without the most recent version of the product, searching outside of Tableau can result in frustration as online searches generally provide information about the most recent version. Further, users often do not immediately recall the exact version they use, thus leading to more difficulty when they seek product-specific help on Tableau's Help pages.



Tableau was interested in improving user help search experience and wanted more information about how users seek help to inform this work.

RESEARCH QUESTIONS

How do Tableau users find help?

  1. What are the pros and cons of each path?
  2. Do search methods differ by user experience level?
  3. How do users identify the version number?
  4. What are the pain points for identifying the version number?

MY ROLE

Usability Tester

TIMEFRAME

10 weeks (Winter 2019)

Methods

RECRUITMENT

Participants were recruited through word-of-mouth and social media posts.


PARTICIPANTS

In total, 10 individuals volunteered to join the study. Given that 5 participants is generally considered the minimum for usability research of this kind, this sample size can be viewed as more than adequate.


Participants

10 adults

Demographics

Testing Environment

Tableau Expertise

Usage

2 professional

TASKS AND METRICS

Pre-testing interviews: during the first portion of the testing, we engaged participants in open-ended questions about their general methods of searching for help when using the software. We evaluated these data by gleaning and analyzing the common themes and frequencies.


Think aloud testing: the next portion of the testing consisted of unobtrusively observing participants complete the tasks while describing their actions and thoughts. We noted the pain points and decisions made.


Tasks

Metrics

1. "Imagine you have been trying to figure out how to [Tableau task] and you need help. Please find the answer the way you normally would for software of this type."

(a) For users familiar with multiple Tableau products, ask whether their process changes by product.

(b) For users familiar with competitors' products (e.g. Microsoft Power BI), ask about their process for similar tasks.


2. "Please identify what Tableau version you are using."

  1. Task completion (dichotomous yes/no)
  2. Task completion time (in seconds)
  3. False starts/attempts per task (numeric quantity)
  4. Paths to task completion (notation of user journey)
  5. Notation of pain points - moments and decisions that seemed to impede users' success (list of design features)
  6. Notation of what went well

Participatory design: after the testing, we interviewed participants to understand how they felt about the tasks (e.g. task difficulty level, satisfaction with the results, etc.) and asked them to share their suggestions on how they would prefer to get help.

Findings

HELP PATHS


No 1. Tableau Official Help Page via Google

  1. Liked the step-to-step previews from Google
  2. Liked the combination of words and graphics
  3. Perception of accuracy and trustworthiness
  1. Users didn't understand Help page terminology

      E.g. create a Sheet Selector for a dashboard


  2. Scattered help content (concept definitions aren't listed, requiring additional help search)

      E.g. create a backup using the TSM command


  3. Some content pages are text heavy

      E.g. backup Tableau Server data


  4. Content isn't easy to follow (lack of easy-to-follow steps, short videos, etc.)

      E.g. backup Tableau Server data

No 2. Google → Tableau Community Forums

  1. Users related to forum participants

    "I can follow people's thoughts. They've struggled with the same issue and resolved it."


  2. Users embraced the collaboration aspect

    "[Forums] feel like a collaboration process."
  1. Not perceived as ultimately trustworthy  ⃰
  2. May take time to find the answer  ⃰
  3. May contain incorrect/outdated solutions  ⃰
  4. Contain specialized jargon/terminology unfamiliar to novice users  ⃰

    5.     ⃰ Usability team's observation

No 3. Video Tutorials → YouTube / Tableau / 3rd Parties

  1. Some users were pleased when they found video tutorials in search results

    "Google is the biggest source [for help], and even better is YouTube."


    "Usually I just like to go and find instructional videos, sometimes through third parties like Lynda."


  2. Tutorials explain and simultaneously demo the process step-by-step, which appeals to visual learners

    "Someone talks about the process AND shows it."


  3. Tutorials frequently cover the content in-depth and provide explanations for workflows

    "Nobody has time to read."
  1. Some users indicated that videos require too large a time investment

    "The thing about the videos is that you have to commit."


  2. A particular topic may be covered mid-way through the video, making within-the-video search time-consuming

    "You can't skip to the end [of a video] to make sure you are achieving the outcome you want."


  3. Not every video contains an easy-to-search through transcript  ⃰

    4.     ⃰ Usability team's observation

No 4. Tableau's Built-in Help Function

  1. Ideally a first path containing helpful links and topics of interest

  2. Strengths include navigation functionality
  1. The greatest challenge seems to be building confidence in the Help function, as users did not expect it to work well and as such did not even try it

    "I feel like it's a last-case resort [to use the Help menu]".


    "Usually Help is not very useful. And I wouldn't expect it to be powerful."


  2. Several users indicated that they rarely use built-in Help functions in any software

    "I have never used Help in any application."


  3. Users' mental model isn't aligned with the intended in-built Help usage

    "I only used built-in Help back in the day, before the internet."

TABLEAU VERSION IDENTIFICATION

6 users identified their version within 30 seconds without prompting. 4 users took longer than 30 seconds or failed to recognize it.


Tableau Version Identification

  1. Version number shows up on hovering

    "I knew it from before [previous experience]."


  2. Standardized placement

    "This thing is standardized, so it's easy."
  1. Not recognizable

    "This is a very poor way to show the version. I thought it was today's date.”

      E.g. 2019.1.0(20191.19.0126.2241)64-bit


  2. Lack of the word "version" or "v"

    "I think it's this one, but it doesn't say 'version', so I'm not sure."

Recommendations


Usability Issues


  1. Unfamiliar terminology

Recommendations


  1. Introduce an Onboarding Experience covering commonly used terminology

  2. Provide an easily accessible "cheat sheet" for users while they are using the software

  3. Clarify unfamiliar terminology used in Tableau Help pages

  1. Built-in Help function's search results do not meet users' expectations

  1. Optimize search engine within Tableau Help

    (a) Synonyms

    (b) Predictions

    (c) Links to relevant content (videos, forums)

  1. Icons not always intuitive

  1. Introduce gifs/rich tool tips upon hovering

      E.g. Photoshop

  1. Users may not find or recognize a version number

  1. Add the word "version" or "v" to clarify

      E.g. Version: 2019.1.0(20191.19.0126.2241)64-bit

  1. Unclear how to find Help content for a particular operating system

  1. Clearly label operating systems
  2. Allow users to select their operating system and version

      E.g. Microsoft Help page

Limitations and Next Steps


Limitations

Next Steps
  1. Convenience sample of mostly student participants
  1. Incentivize a small pool of Tableau users within various industries and roles to participate in further, more targeted testing
  1. Due to differences in participant experience level, we did not use the same tasks for all participants
  1. Select a more standardized set of tasks in subsequent evaluations
  1. For some participants, help search while being observed may differ from help search when alone
  1. Use unmoderated testing for subsequent evaluation (e.g. video diaries)
  1. Most participants were familiar with and tested on Tableau Desktop
  1. It could be helpful to observe Help search behavior for other Tableau products
